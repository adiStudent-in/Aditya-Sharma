Chapter 1 – Introduction to Microeconomics

Class 11 Economics · Microeconomics

What is an Economy?

An economy is a system by which a society produces, distributes, and consumes goods and services to satisfy human wants. It deals with how people make choices under conditions of scarcity.

Key Economic Agents

AgentRole
HouseholdsConsumers of goods; suppliers of factors of production
FirmsProducers of goods and services; employers of factors
GovernmentRegulator, producer, consumer; provides public goods
External SectorInternational trade and capital flows

Scarcity and Choice

Scarcity is the fundamental economic problem — human wants are unlimited but resources are limited.

Key Concepts

  • Scarcity – Limited availability of resources relative to unlimited wants
  • Choice – Selecting among alternatives due to scarcity
  • Resource Allocation – Deciding how to distribute scarce resources

Types of Resources

FactorDescriptionReward

LabourHuman effort (physical and mental)Wages
CapitalMan-made goods (machinery, tools, buildings)Interest
EntrepreneurshipOrganises other factors; takes riskProfit

Central Problems of an Economy

Every economy must answer three fundamental questions:

ProblemQuestionExample
What to produce?Which goods and in what quantities?More consumer goods vs capital goods
How to produce?Which production technique?Labour-intensive vs capital-intensive
For whom to produce?How to distribute output?Based on income, need, or entitlement

These problems arise due to scarcity of resources relative to unlimited wants.

Opportunity Cost

Opportunity cost is the value of the next best alternative foregone when a choice is made.

Example: If you spend ₹500 on a book instead of a movie ticket, the opportunity cost is the enjoyment of the movie you gave up.

Key Points

  • Applies to all economic decisions (individual, firm, government)
  • Increases as more of a good is produced (increasing opportunity cost)
  • Zero opportunity cost only when resources are abundant (unrealistic)

Production Possibility Curve (PPC)

PPC shows the maximum combinations of two goods that can be produced with available resources and technology.

Properties of PPC

PropertyExplanation
Downward slopingTo produce more of one good, some of the other must be sacrificed
Concave to originIncreasing opportunity cost — diminishing returns
Points on the curveEfficient utilisation of resources
Points inside the curveUnderutilisation of resources
Points outside the curveUnattainable with current resources/technology

Shifts in PPC

  • Rightward shift – Economic growth (↑ resources, better technology)
  • Leftward shift – Economic decline (↓ resources, natural disasters)
  • Rotational shift – Improvement in technology for only one good

Types of Economic Systems

1. Centrally Planned Economy (Socialist)

  • Government decides what, how, and for whom to produce
  • No private property; collective ownership
  • Examples: China, Cuba, North Korea

2. Market Economy (Capitalist)

  • Decisions driven by price mechanism (demand and supply)
  • Private property; profit motive; minimal government role
  • Examples: USA, UK, Japan

3. Mixed Economy

  • Combination of market and government decisions
  • Private sector + public sector coexist
  • Examples: India, Brazil, Germany
